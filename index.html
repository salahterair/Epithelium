Body Tissues

Structural organization, epithelial tissue, connective tissue, muscle tissue, nervous tissue, and clinically relevant tissue repair.

1. Learning Objectives
  • Define a tissue as a group of similar cells organized to perform a common function.
  • Explain how tissues fit into the hierarchy from molecules and cells to organs and body systems.
  • Identify the four major tissue types: epithelial, connective, muscle, and nervous tissue.
  • Classify epithelial tissue according to cell layers and cell shape.
  • Classify connective tissue into connective tissue proper and specialized connective tissues.
  • Relate tissue structure to normal function, disease, repair, and selected clinical examples.
Key concept: Anatomy becomes clinically meaningful when structure is linked to function. Normal tissue organization supports normal organ function, while abnormal tissue structure may produce disease.
2. Why Body Tissues Matter in Anatomy

Body tissues are the basic working materials from which organs are built. A blood vessel, bone, stomach wall, nerve, and skin surface all contain tissues arranged in a specific pattern. Understanding tissues helps Year 1 students move from gross anatomical structures to the microscopic basis of organ function.

Key concept: A tissue is not just a collection of cells. It is a functional arrangement of cells and extracellular material that performs a defined task.
Clinical link: Many diseases begin as tissue-level changes. For example, epithelial injury may lead to ulceration, connective tissue weakness may affect support, and nervous tissue damage may impair sensation or movement.
3. Hierarchy of Structural Organization

The human body is organized from smaller to larger levels. Molecules form organelles; organelles are found within cells; similar cells form tissues; multiple tissues combine to form organs; organs work together in systems; systems maintain the body as a whole.

LevelDescriptionExample
Chemical levelAtoms and moleculesCalcium ions, proteins, lipids
Cellular levelCells and their organellesSmooth muscle cell, neuron
Tissue levelSimilar cells performing a common functionEpithelial tissue, connective tissue
Organ levelMore than one tissue forming a discrete structureHeart, lung, stomach, skin
System levelOrgans working together for a common purposeCardiovascular, respiratory, gastrointestinal systems
Key concept: The lecture source emphasizes the sequence cells → tissues → organs → systems → body as a core organizing framework.
4. Normal Structure, Normal Function, and Health

Normal molecules support normal cell structure. Normal cells maintain normal tissue architecture. Normal tissues support normal organ action, and normal organs maintain system function. This chain explains why anatomical structure and physiological function should be studied together.

Clinical link: Disease often reflects disruption of this chain. A molecular abnormality may change cell behavior, a cell abnormality may alter tissue structure, and tissue dysfunction may produce organ failure.
5. The Four Basic Tissue Types

The body contains four major tissue families: epithelial tissue, connective tissue, muscle tissue, and nervous tissue. Each family has a dominant functional role, but organs usually contain several tissue types working together.

Tissue typeMain structural ideaMajor functionsExamples
Epithelial tissueClosely packed cells covering surfaces or forming glandsProtection, absorption, secretion, filtration, excretion, sensationEpidermis, intestinal lining, glands
Connective tissueCells separated by extracellular matrixSupport, binding, protection, storage, transportAreolar tissue, adipose tissue, cartilage, bone, blood
Muscle tissueElongated contractile cellsMovement, posture, propulsion, pumpingSkeletal, cardiac, smooth muscle
Nervous tissueExcitable cells and supporting cellsCommunication, control, response to stimuliBrain, spinal cord, peripheral nerves
6. Epithelial Tissue: General Features

Epithelial tissue covers body surfaces, lines cavities, forms glands, and separates compartments. It is characterized by tightly packed cells, little extracellular matrix, polarity, attachment to a basement membrane, absence of blood vessels, and a strong capacity for regeneration.

Key concept: Epithelium is avascular, so nutrients reach epithelial cells by diffusion from underlying connective tissue.
Clinical link: Because epithelial surfaces face friction, chemicals, microbes, and dehydration, epithelial tissues are common sites of inflammation, metaplasia, ulcers, and carcinoma.
7. Functions of Epithelial Tissue

Epithelial function depends on its location and structure. Thick stratified epithelium protects against abrasion. Thin simple squamous epithelium allows diffusion and filtration. Simple columnar epithelium with microvilli supports absorption and secretion. Glandular epithelium produces secretions.

FunctionStructural requirementExample
ProtectionMultiple layers and strong cell junctionsSkin epidermis, oral mucosa
AbsorptionThin epithelium with apical specializationsSmall intestine
SecretionSecretory epithelial cells or glandsSalivary glands, gastric glands
FiltrationVery thin epithelial barrierRenal corpuscle regions
SensationAssociation with sensory receptorsNasal mucosa, skin
8. Classification of Epithelium by Layers

Epithelial tissue is first classified by the number of cell layers. Simple epithelium has one layer of cells and is commonly adapted for absorption, secretion, diffusion, or filtration. Stratified epithelium has multiple layers and is mainly adapted for protection.

Layer patternDefinitionFunctional meaning
SimpleOne cell layer; every cell rests on the basement membraneEfficient exchange, absorption, secretion, filtration
StratifiedTwo or more layers; only basal cells contact the basement membraneMechanical protection against friction and injury
PseudostratifiedAppears multilayered, but all cells contact the basement membraneOften involved in secretion and movement of mucus
TransitionalStratified epithelium that changes shape with stretchAllows urinary organs to distend
Classification rule: Name epithelial tissue by layers first, then by the shape of the surface cells.
9. Classification of Epithelium by Cell Shape

The second part of epithelial classification is based on the shape of the cells at the free surface. Squamous cells are flat, cuboidal cells are roughly cube-shaped, and columnar cells are taller than they are wide.

Cell shapeAppearanceTypical role
SquamousThin, flat cellsDiffusion, filtration, smooth lining
CuboidalCells with similar height and widthSecretion and absorption
ColumnarTall cells with basal nucleiAbsorption, secretion, protection
Clinical link: When surface epithelium is repeatedly irritated, adaptive change may occur. For example, chronic irritation can alter epithelial phenotype and increase disease risk.
10. Glandular Epithelium

Glandular epithelium is specialized for secretion. Endocrine glands are ductless and release hormones into blood or tissue fluid. Exocrine glands use ducts to deliver secretions onto body surfaces or into body cavities.

Gland typeDuctsProduct pathwayExample
EndocrineAbsentHormones enter blood or interstitial fluidThyroid gland
ExocrinePresentSecretion reaches epithelial surface through ductsSalivary, sweat, sebaceous glands
Pharmacology link: Many drugs act on epithelial secretions. Anticholinergic drugs may reduce salivary secretion, causing dry mouth, while some respiratory drugs modify mucus production or clearance.
11. Connective Tissue: General Features

Connective tissue supports, binds, protects, stores, and transports. Unlike epithelium, connective tissue usually contains fewer cells and more extracellular matrix. The matrix includes fibers and ground substance, and its composition determines tissue strength, flexibility, and function.

Key concept: Connective tissue is defined by extracellular matrix. The matrix may be soft and hydrated, firm and flexible, hard and calcified, or fluid.
12. Classification of Connective Tissue

Connective tissue can be organized into connective tissue proper and specialized connective tissues. Connective tissue proper includes loose areolar, dense, reticular, and adipose tissue. Specialized connective tissue includes cartilage, bone, and blood.

Main groupTypesMajor role
Connective tissue properLoose areolar, dense, reticular, adiposeBinding, packing, support, storage
Specialized connective tissueCartilage, bone, bloodFlexible support, rigid support, transport
Clinical link: Connective tissue disorders may present with joint laxity, fragile skin, poor wound healing, abnormal bleeding, or bone fragility depending on the affected component.
13. Loose Areolar Connective Tissue

Loose areolar connective tissue is a flexible packing tissue found beneath epithelia and around blood vessels and nerves. It contains cells, collagen fibers, elastic fibers, and ground substance. It allows diffusion, provides support, and participates in inflammation and tissue repair.

Key concept: Areolar tissue often forms the supportive layer under epithelial tissue, making it important for epithelial nutrition and repair.
14. Dense Connective Tissue

Dense connective tissue contains abundant collagen fibers and provides tensile strength. Dense regular connective tissue has parallel collagen bundles, as in tendons and ligaments. Dense irregular connective tissue has collagen arranged in multiple directions, as in the dermis and organ capsules.

TypeFiber arrangementFunctional meaningExample
Dense regularParallel collagen bundlesResists force in one main directionTendon, ligament
Dense irregularInterwoven collagen bundlesResists stress from many directionsDermis, fibrous capsules
15. Reticular and Adipose Tissue

Reticular connective tissue forms a delicate supporting framework in lymphoid organs and bone marrow. Adipose tissue is a loose connective tissue composed mainly of lipid-storing adipocytes. It stores energy, cushions organs, provides insulation, and contributes to endocrine regulation.

Clinical link: The lecture source identifies adipose tissue as loose connective tissue with large lipid-storing cells. In clinical anatomy, fat distribution affects body habitus, surgical planes, and metabolic risk.
Pharmacology link: Lipid-soluble drugs may distribute into adipose tissue, which can influence apparent volume of distribution and duration of action.
16. Cartilage

Cartilage is a specialized connective tissue with a firm, flexible extracellular matrix. Chondrocytes lie in lacunae, and cartilage generally lacks blood vessels. Because it is avascular, repair is slower than in vascular connective tissues.

Cartilage typeMain featureExample
Hyaline cartilageSmooth glassy matrix; supports and reduces frictionArticular cartilage, costal cartilage, tracheal rings
Elastic cartilageElastic fibers provide flexibilityAuricle, epiglottis
FibrocartilageStrong collagen-rich cartilageIntervertebral discs, pubic symphysis
Clinical link: Damage to articular cartilage can cause pain and reduced joint movement because cartilage has limited regenerative capacity.
17. Bone or Osseous Tissue

Bone is a specialized connective tissue with cells, fibers, ground substance, and a calcified extracellular matrix. The mineralized matrix gives bone rigidity and enables support, protection, leverage for movement, mineral storage, and housing of bone marrow.

FunctionAnatomical meaning
SupportForms an internal framework for the body
AttachmentProvides surfaces for muscles and tendons
ProtectionProtects cranial and thoracic organs
Mineral reservoirStores calcium and phosphate
HemopoiesisContains marrow spaces for blood cell formation
Key concept: Bone is connective tissue, but its extracellular matrix is calcified.
18. Macroscopic Structure of a Long Bone

A long bone is organized into regions. The diaphysis is the shaft. The epiphyses are the ends. The metaphysis lies between the shaft and the end. In growing bones, the epiphyseal plate is the growth region. The medullary cavity contains marrow, while trabeculae form a lattice of spongy bone.

TermMeaning
DiaphysisShaft of a long bone
EpiphysisEnd of a long bone
MetaphysisRegion between diaphysis and epiphysis
Epiphyseal plateGrowth plate in immature bone
Medullary cavityCentral marrow cavity
TrabeculaeThin bony columns forming spongy bone
Articular cartilageHyaline cartilage covering joint surfaces
19. Bone Coverings: Periosteum and Endosteum

The periosteum is the external covering of bone, except over articular cartilage and some tendon or ligament insertion areas. It is important for growth, repair, vascular supply, and tendon or ligament attachment. The endosteum lines internal bone surfaces, including marrow spaces and osteonic canals.

Clinical link: The periosteum is pain-sensitive. In fractures or inflammation, periosteal irritation contributes significantly to bone pain.
20. Blood as a Connective Tissue

Blood is a specialized connective tissue with a fluid extracellular matrix called plasma. It contains erythrocytes, leukocytes, and platelets. It also contains fibrinogen, a soluble protein that can be converted into fibrin during blood clotting.

ComponentMain role
ErythrocytesTransport oxygen and carbon dioxide
LeukocytesDefense and immune response
PlateletsHemostasis and clot formation
PlasmaFluid medium for transport of cells, proteins, nutrients, hormones, and waste
FibrinogenPotential fiber-forming protein converted to fibrin during clotting
Clinical link: Anemia, leukocytosis, thrombocytopenia, and clotting disorders are examples of clinical problems involving blood as connective tissue.
21. Muscle Tissue

Muscle tissue is specialized for contraction. It is made of elongated cells that generate force and allow body movement, organ movement, and blood pumping. The lecture source highlights three important muscle properties: excitability, extensibility, and elasticity.

PropertyMeaning
ExcitabilityAbility to receive and respond to a stimulus
ExtensibilityAbility to be stretched
ElasticityAbility to return toward original shape after stretching or contraction
22. Three Types of Muscle Tissue

Muscle tissue is classified into skeletal, cardiac, and smooth muscle. Skeletal muscle is striated and voluntary. Cardiac muscle is striated and involuntary. Smooth muscle is non-striated and involuntary.

TypeStriationsControlLocation
Skeletal musclePresentVoluntaryAttached to skeleton, facial muscles, diaphragm
Cardiac musclePresentInvoluntaryMyocardium of the heart
Smooth muscleAbsentInvoluntaryWalls of hollow organs and blood vessels
Clinical link: Damage to skeletal muscle affects movement, damage to cardiac muscle affects pumping, and abnormal smooth muscle contraction may affect airway diameter, intestinal motility, or vascular tone.
Pharmacology link: Many drugs target smooth muscle contraction, such as bronchodilators in airway disease and vasodilators in vascular conditions.
23. Nervous Tissue

Nervous tissue is specialized for irritability and conductivity. It includes neurons, which transmit electrical signals, and supporting neuroglial cells. It is found in the brain, spinal cord, and peripheral nerves. Nervous tissue carries information from sensory receptors to the central nervous system and from the central nervous system to effectors such as muscles and glands.

Key concept: Nervous tissue provides rapid communication and coordination across body systems.
Clinical link: Nerve injury may produce sensory loss, weakness, abnormal reflexes, or autonomic dysfunction depending on the type and location of damaged fibers.
24. Tissue Repair and Regeneration

Tissue repair depends on the type of tissue, the degree of injury, blood supply, and regenerative capacity. Epithelial tissue usually regenerates well because epithelial cells divide actively. Connective tissue contributes to scar formation. Cartilage repairs slowly because it is avascular. Nervous tissue in the central nervous system has limited regenerative capacity.

TissueTypical repair capacityReason
EpitheliumGoodFrequent mitosis and stem cell renewal
Connective tissueVariableDepends on vascularity and matrix
CartilagePoorAvascular matrix limits repair
BoneGood when aligned and vascularizedActive remodeling and blood supply
Nervous tissueLimited, especially in CNSLow neuronal replacement and inhibitory environment
Clinical link: Wound healing often restores continuity, but not always the original tissue architecture. Scar tissue may be strong but less specialized than the tissue it replaces.
25. Clinical Correlations and Review Questions
Clinical correlation: Epithelial ulceration reflects loss of surface lining and can occur in the gastrointestinal tract or skin. Cartilage injury may persist because cartilage is avascular. Bone fracture repair is more effective when blood supply and alignment are preserved. Nerve injury can cause loss of sensation or motor function.

Estimated reading time: 12–15 minutes

1. From Cells to Tissues and Organs

The study of body tissues begins with the idea that the body is organized in levels. Molecules and organelles support the life of cells, while groups of similar cells cooperate to form a tissue. A tissue is therefore a functional unit, not merely a collection of cells. When several tissues are arranged together, they form an organ, and organs cooperate within body systems to support life.

This hierarchy explains why anatomy is clinically important. A small disturbance at the cellular level may produce a tissue abnormality, and a tissue abnormality may alter organ function. In the healthy body, structure and function are aligned; in disease, structural change often produces functional loss.

Key concept: Tissues connect microscopic anatomy with gross anatomy because organs are built from organized combinations of tissue types.

2. Epithelial Tissue as a Surface and Glandular Tissue

Epithelial tissue covers surfaces, lines cavities, and forms glands. Its cells are closely packed and supported by a basement membrane. Because epithelium is avascular, it depends on diffusion from the underlying connective tissue for nutrition. This arrangement is suitable for protection, absorption, secretion, filtration, excretion, and sensation.

Epithelial classification depends first on the number of layers and then on the shape of the surface cells. Simple epithelium is usually adapted for exchange, absorption, or secretion, while stratified epithelium is usually adapted for protection. Glandular epithelium forms endocrine glands, which release hormones without ducts, and exocrine glands, which deliver secretions through ducts.

Clinical Correlation: Epithelial surfaces are common sites of ulceration, infection, irritation, and neoplasia because they are exposed to mechanical and chemical stress.
Common exam confusion: Stratified epithelium is named according to the shape of the cells at the free surface, not the basal layer.

3. Connective Tissue and the Importance of Matrix

Connective tissue supports, binds, protects, stores, and transports. Its defining feature is the extracellular matrix, which may be soft, firm, calcified, or fluid. Connective tissue proper includes loose areolar tissue, dense connective tissue, reticular tissue, and adipose tissue. Specialized connective tissues include cartilage, bone, and blood.

Adipose tissue consists mainly of lipid-storing cells and acts as an energy store, cushion, and insulator. Cartilage provides firm but flexible support, especially in joints and respiratory passages. Bone has a calcified matrix that provides support, protection, mineral storage, and a site for blood cell formation. Blood is also connective tissue because its cells are suspended in a fluid extracellular matrix called plasma.

Key concept: The physical character of connective tissue depends more on its extracellular matrix than on cell crowding.
Clinical Correlation: Cartilage has limited repair capacity because it lacks blood vessels, while bone can repair effectively if blood supply and alignment are preserved.

4. Bone, Blood, and Structural Support

Bone is a specialized connective tissue composed of cells, fibers, ground substance, and mineralized matrix. In a long bone, the diaphysis forms the shaft, the epiphyses form the ends, and the metaphysis lies between them. The medullary cavity contains marrow, and trabeculae form the internal lattice of spongy bone.

The periosteum covers the external bone surface except where articular cartilage is present, while the endosteum lines internal bone surfaces. Blood, although fluid, belongs to the connective tissue family because plasma functions as its extracellular matrix and its cells include erythrocytes, leukocytes, and platelets.

Clinical Correlation: Periosteal irritation is an important source of pain in fractures because the periosteum is richly innervated.
Common exam confusion: Blood is not epithelial tissue and not simply a fluid; it is a specialized connective tissue with plasma as its matrix.

5. Muscle and Nervous Tissue as Excitable Tissues

Muscle tissue is specialized for contraction. Skeletal muscle is striated and voluntary, cardiac muscle is striated and involuntary, and smooth muscle is non-striated and involuntary. The important properties of muscle include excitability, extensibility, and elasticity. These properties allow muscle to respond to stimuli, stretch, and return toward its original shape.

Nervous tissue is specialized for irritability and conductivity. It is found in the brain, spinal cord, and nerves, and it transmits signals from sensory receptors to the central nervous system and from the central nervous system to effectors such as muscles and glands. Together, muscle and nervous tissue allow movement, coordination, and rapid response to the environment.

Clinical Correlation: Damage to nervous tissue may cause sensory loss, weakness, or altered reflexes, while damage to muscle tissue may impair movement, posture, breathing, or cardiac pumping depending on the type involved.
