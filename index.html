Epithelium

Characteristics, classification, surface specializations, glands & clinical correlations

1. Learning Objectives
  • Define epithelial tissue and describe its general organization.
  • Explain the main characteristics of epithelial cells: polarity, junctions, basement membrane attachment, avascularity, and regeneration.
  • Classify covering epithelia according to number of layers and shape of surface cells.
  • Recognize the main types of simple, stratified, pseudostratified, and transitional epithelium.
  • Relate epithelial structure to function in common organs.
  • Describe the basic classification of glandular epithelium.
2. What Is Epithelium?

Epithelium is a tissue composed of closely packed cells that cover external body surfaces, line internal cavities and tubes, and form the secretory parts of glands.

Core idea: Epithelium is the body’s surface and lining tissue. It forms selective barriers between the external environment, body cavities, and underlying connective tissue.

4. General Characteristics of Epithelium
  • High cellularity: cells are very close together with little extracellular matrix.
  • Polarity: each cell has apical, lateral, and basal domains.
  • Specialized junctions: cells are attached to each other by junctional complexes.
  • Basement membrane: basal surface is supported by a basement membrane.
  • Avascularity: epithelium contains no blood vessels.
  • Regeneration: epithelial cells commonly show active mitosis and renewal.
5. Cell Polarity

Epithelial cells are polarized, meaning different parts of the same cell have different structures and functions.

DomainFacesCommon Features
ApicalLumen or external surfaceMicrovilli, cilia, stereocilia
LateralNeighboring cellsTight junctions, adherens junctions, desmosomes, gap junctions
BasalConnective tissueBasement membrane attachment, hemidesmosomes
Practical point: Always identify the free apical surface first when looking at epithelium under the microscope.
6. Basement Membrane

The basement membrane is a thin extracellular layer between epithelium and connective tissue. It supports epithelial cells and helps control cell attachment, growth, repair, and filtration.

ComponentMain SourceKey Molecules
Basal laminaEpithelial cellsLaminin and type IV collagen

7. Avascularity and Nutrition

Epithelium is avascular. It receives oxygen and nutrients by diffusion from blood vessels in the underlying connective tissue.

Clinical link: Epithelial injury heals efficiently when the basement membrane and underlying connective tissue remain intact, because surviving epithelial cells can migrate and divide.

11. Basis of Classification

Covering epithelium is classified using two criteria:

  1. Number of cell layers: simple, stratified, or pseudostratified.
  2. Shape of surface cells: squamous, cuboidal, or columnar.
Rule: In stratified epithelium, classification is based on the shape of the surface cells, not the deepest cells.
12. Simple Epithelia

Simple epithelium has one layer of cells. Because every cell touches the basement membrane, simple epithelia are usually specialized for diffusion, filtration, absorption, and secretion rather than strong mechanical protection.

TypeMain FunctionCommon Location
Simple squamousRapid diffusion and filtrationAlveoli, endothelium, mesothelium
Simple cuboidalSecretion and absorptionKidney tubules, thyroid follicles
Simple columnarAbsorption and secretionStomach, intestine, gallbladder
13. Simple Squamous Epithelium

Simple squamous epithelium consists of a single layer of flat cells with flattened nuclei. It is very thin, making it ideal for diffusion, filtration, and smooth lining surfaces.

  • Alveoli: gas exchange.
  • Endothelium: lining of blood and lymph vessels.
  • Mesothelium: lining of pleural, pericardial, and peritoneal cavities.
14. Simple Cuboidal Epithelium

Simple cuboidal epithelium consists of one layer of cube-shaped cells with centrally placed round nuclei. It is commonly involved in secretion and absorption.

Examples: Kidney tubules, thyroid follicles, small ducts of exocrine glands, and the surface of the ovary.
15. Simple Columnar Epithelium

Simple columnar epithelium consists of tall cells with oval nuclei usually located near the basal region. It is common in organs specialized for absorption and secretion.

  • Intestine: absorptive cells with microvilli and goblet cells.
  • Stomach: mucus-secreting columnar cells.
  • Gallbladder: absorptive columnar epithelium.
16. Pseudostratified Columnar Epithelium

Pseudostratified epithelium appears multilayered because nuclei are at different levels, but all cells contact the basement membrane. The classic example is respiratory epithelium.

FeatureSignificance
CiliaMove mucus toward the pharynx
Goblet cellsSecrete mucus to trap dust and microbes
Basal cellsStem cells for epithelial renewal
17. Stratified Squamous Epithelium

Stratified squamous epithelium has many layers and flat surface cells. Its main function is protection against friction and mechanical injury.

TypeSurface CellsLocationMain Protection
KeratinizedDead, keratin-filled cellsEpidermisAbrasion and dehydration
Non-keratinizedLiving moist cellsOral cavity, esophagus, vaginaAbrasion in moist surfaces
18. Transitional Epithelium

Transitional epithelium, also called urothelium, lines the urinary tract. It is specialized for distension and forms a barrier against the toxic effect of urine.

  • Found in the renal calyces, renal pelvis, ureter, urinary bladder, and proximal urethra.
  • Surface umbrella cells appear dome-shaped when relaxed and flatter when stretched.
  • It prevents urine leakage into underlying tissue.
19. Glandular Epithelium

Glandular epithelium is specialized for secretion. Glands may be unicellular, such as goblet cells, or multicellular, such as salivary glands.

Goblet cell: A unicellular exocrine gland that secretes mucus.

21. Clinical Correlations
Metaplasia: Chronic irritation may cause replacement of one mature epithelial type by another. For example, smoking can replace ciliated respiratory epithelium with stratified squamous epithelium, reducing mucociliary clearance.
Carcinoma: Malignant tumors arising from epithelium are called carcinomas. Squamous cell carcinoma and adenocarcinoma are common examples.
Ciliary dysfunction: Defective ciliary movement impairs mucus clearance and may cause recurrent respiratory infection.
22. How to Identify Epithelium in Practical Histology
  1. Find the free surface or lumen.
  2. Count the number of cell layers.
  3. Identify the shape of the surface cells.
  4. Look for apical specializations such as cilia or microvilli.
  5. Relate the epithelial type to its function and location.
Example: A single layer of tall cells with goblet cells and brush border in the intestine is simple columnar epithelium specialized for absorption and secretion.

1. Overview of Epithelial Tissue

Epithelium is one of the basic tissues of the body. It forms the cellular covering of external surfaces, lines internal cavities and tubes, and forms the secretory component of glands. Because epithelial cells are closely packed, this tissue is well suited to form selective barriers between the external environment, body spaces, and underlying connective tissue.

The functions of epithelium depend on its structure. Thin epithelium favors diffusion and filtration, while multilayered epithelium provides protection. Columnar and cuboidal cells are commonly involved in secretion and absorption, especially when their apical surface is modified by microvilli or cilia.

Key concept: Always connect epithelial structure with function. A thin lining usually supports exchange; a thick multilayered lining usually supports protection.

2. Main Characteristics of Epithelium

Epithelial tissue is characterized by high cellularity, meaning that cells are closely arranged with very little extracellular matrix. The cells show polarity, with distinct apical, lateral, and basal domains. The apical surface faces a lumen or external surface, while the basal surface rests on the basement membrane.

Epithelium is usually avascular, so it receives oxygen and nutrients by diffusion from blood vessels in the underlying connective tissue. Many epithelia also have a high capacity for regeneration, which is important because epithelial surfaces are frequently exposed to mechanical stress, chemical irritation, and microbial contact.

Clinical Correlation: Superficial epithelial injury may heal efficiently if the basement membrane and underlying connective tissue remain intact, because surviving epithelial cells can migrate and divide.

3. Basement Membrane and Cell Junctions

The basement membrane is a thin extracellular layer that supports epithelium and separates it from connective tissue. It includes the basal lamina, produced mainly by epithelial cells, and the reticular lamina, contributed by connective tissue. Important molecules include laminin, type IV collagen, and type III collagen.

Epithelial cells are joined by specialized junctions. Tight junctions seal the space between cells, desmosomes resist mechanical stress, gap junctions allow communication, and hemidesmosomes anchor epithelial cells to the basement membrane.

Common exam confusion: Desmosomes attach cells to neighboring cells, while hemidesmosomes attach epithelial cells to the basement membrane.

4. Classification of Covering Epithelium

Covering epithelium is classified using two criteria: the number of layers and the shape of the surface cells. Simple epithelium has one layer of cells. Stratified epithelium has two or more layers. Pseudostratified epithelium appears multilayered, but all cells touch the basement membrane.

The surface cell shape may be squamous, cuboidal, or columnar. In stratified epithelium, classification is based on the surface cells, not the basal cells. Therefore, many layers with flat surface cells are called stratified squamous epithelium.

Histology identification tip: Find the free surface first, count the layers, then identify the shape of the surface cells.

5. Simple Epithelia

Simple squamous epithelium is very thin and is ideal for diffusion and filtration. It is found in alveoli, blood vessels as endothelium, and serous cavities as mesothelium. Simple cuboidal epithelium is commonly involved in secretion and absorption, such as in kidney tubules and thyroid follicles.

Simple columnar epithelium consists of tall cells and is common in the gastrointestinal tract. In the intestine, it supports absorption and secretion, often with microvilli and goblet cells. In the stomach, columnar epithelial cells are specialized mainly for mucus secretion and mucosal protection.

Clinical Correlation: Damage to intestinal epithelium may impair absorption because the absorptive surface and brush border are essential for nutrient uptake.

6. Pseudostratified, Stratified, and Transitional Epithelium

Pseudostratified columnar epithelium is classically found in the respiratory tract. Its nuclei appear at different levels, giving a false impression of multiple layers. Respiratory epithelium commonly contains cilia, goblet cells, and basal cells. Cilia move mucus, goblet cells secrete mucus, and basal cells contribute to renewal.

Stratified squamous epithelium is adapted for protection against abrasion. It may be keratinized, as in the epidermis, or non-keratinized, as in the oral cavity and esophagus. Transitional epithelium, or urothelium, lines much of the urinary tract and allows stretching while maintaining a barrier against urine.

Clinical Correlation: Chronic smoking may cause metaplasia of respiratory epithelium, replacing ciliated pseudostratified epithelium with stratified squamous epithelium and reducing mucociliary clearance.

7. Glandular Epithelium

Glandular epithelium is specialized for secretion. Exocrine glands secrete through ducts onto a surface or into a lumen, while endocrine glands are ductless and release hormones into the bloodstream. Goblet cells are unicellular exocrine glands that secrete mucus.

Exocrine glands may release products by different mechanisms. Merocrine secretion occurs by exocytosis, apocrine secretion releases part of the apical cytoplasm with the product, and holocrine secretion occurs when the entire cell breaks down to become part of the secretion.

Key concept: Exocrine glands have ducts; endocrine glands do not have ducts and release hormones into blood.

8. Summary for Practical Histology

When examining epithelial tissue under the microscope, first identify the free surface or lumen. Then determine whether the epithelium is simple, stratified, pseudostratified, or transitional. Next, identify the shape of the surface cells and look for specializations such as cilia, microvilli, goblet cells, keratin, or umbrella cells.

Common exam focus: Students are often tested on matching epithelial type with location and function, such as simple squamous epithelium in alveoli, stratified squamous epithelium in the esophagus, and transitional epithelium in the urinary bladder.
